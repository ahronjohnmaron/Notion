Webpage Scorecard

What are some things to look out for when putting together your web pages? Use this as a scorecard or checklist to evaluate your web pages.

The best way to do that is to start with item #1 and look through your webpage from top to bottom for that one item. Then when you're done with that item #2, move on to the next item #2, and go from top to bottom on your webpage again. Repeat the process until you've gone through all items.

What you don't want to do is look at your webpage and then look at all the items on the list at once. You want to take it one item at a time.

1.

Is all the important stuff above the fold as much as possible? Here is an example of all the important items above the fold on desktop. It's a little harder on mobile, but here is an example of getting as much as possible above the fold on mobile.

a. What's the important stuff? Pre-headline, headline, sub-headline/tagline, video, and optin/button.
b. Small logo or no logo at all at the top - Since above the fold is valuable screen real estate, we generally recommend not having your logo or at least making it very small.
2.

How is the padding? In order to get all the important stuff above the fold, your padding (space between sentences and elements) at the top of your webpage (and throughout) should be minimal. Here is an example of minimal padding. Here is an example of too much padding. This is usually an issue on mobile (like the example), but can also happen on desktop.

3.

The pre-headline, headline, and sub-headline/tagline.

a. Pre-headline - We like to use it to quickly say what it is and who it's for: 2-Day Online Workshop for Online Entrepreneurs. Notice we called it an online workshop and not live event or virtual event because we wanted to make it as easy as possible for someone to understand.
b.

Headline - This is the most important text on the webpage because it needs to capture your target prospect's attention to encourage them to continue reading the webpage and follow through on registration. So what makes a good headline?

i. Does it create and evoke curiosity or address a pain point without making them feel insecure and not want to opt in? For example, you might have a weight loss offer, but saying "tired of being fat" might be something the prospect isn't willing to opt in and admit.
ii. Does it hit home for your target audience? Does it speak to them? Is it relatable without being too niched?
iii. Does it address a major concern or objection?
iv. See the Hook & Title Idea Generator for ideas.
c. Sub-headline or tagline - This is where you can expand on the headline and say what you couldn't squeeze in and also reiterate anything important.
d.

Does this text stand out and is it easy to read?

i. In our example, the pre-headline is an orange box with white text. The white text and orange is a decent contrast for the text to be readable. This also makes the pre-headline stand out from the headline.
ii. The headline is in all bold black text (with some underline) and large font. This should make it one of the most prominent things you see and stand out from the pre-headline and sub-headline/tagline.
iii. Another reason the text stands out well is because the black or dark orange text contrasts nicely with the light orange background. Make sure the contrast is there and not something like blue text on light blue background.
iv. Is the text easy to read also applies to text throughout your webpage. What makes it easy to read is the font (use sans-serif and avoid serif, narrow, or decorative fonts), font color that contrasts well against the background, and font size (not too small).
4.

Video

a. Minimal player settings and distractions - The video needs to have minimal settings where all the viewer can do is click play, pause, and replay. Anything else is a distraction. The worst thing you can do is use YouTube to host the video because you have no control on the player controls and someone can click on the video and go to YouTube and fall into that distraction rabbit hole. Using video hosting like Vimeo or Wisita are much better options.
b. Is it set on an attractive thumbnail? - By default, the video will likely use a random frame from the video and it might not be an attractive picture of the speaker talking mid-sentence. Depending on the video host, you should be able to manually select the clip or upload a different screenshot to your liking.
5.

Optin Fields - You can either have the optin fields on the webpage like our SBM example or have it on a popup after they click on the registration button.

a. Asking for all the info you need - At the least, get their name and email. Adding mobile is a good idea so that you can text and call them as additional follow-up on top of email in case they don't book their interview call afterwards.
b. Placeholder text - The placeholder text is the text inside the field where the prospect would enter their information. By default, it'll probably say "Email" in the Email field, but for more advanced settings you can put something like "My best email is..." to encourage people to put their real email and not a throwaway email. In the SBM example, you can see the placeholder text for the mobile field is "Your number (for Special Bonus)..." to encourage them to enter their mobile phone number.
c. We won't spam you or sell your information disclaimer - You don't necessarily need to put this onto your webpage if you have your optin fields on the page itself, but if you're doing a button with a pop-up with the fields, you can add this disclaimer in that window if you'd like to help people feel more comfortable in entering their data.
6.

Registration Button

a. Button content - The content of your button should just be something like "I'm in! Save My Spot" or "Request Your Invitation". It shouldn't have any additional text beyond that.
b. Does the button stand out? The text of the button should contrast well with the button color, which should contrast well with the background so that it stands out and people know that the thing to do on the webpage is to click that button. In the ITfactor example, it's black text on a pink button on a black background. The black text is easy to read and stands out against the pink button and the pink button stands out as the only pink thing on the black background.
c.

Does the button work?

i. Popup - If you don't have the optin fields on the screen like the ITfactor example, the button should click to a popup that asks for the optin information (and then submit through to the call booking page).
ii. Submit and go to the next page - In our SBM example, the optin fields are already on the screen, so the button should submit the info and successfully go to the call booking page next.
iii. After the (final) button is clicked and the prospect's info is submitted, does the person get added to your email list (and tagged) correctly? The data in the optin fields should go to your email marketing service onto the correct list and tagged appropriately so that they can be emailed if they don't book the interview call shortly after.
d. Additional Buttons - If you have a long webpage with multiple sections, you should have additional registration buttons throughout so that people don't have to scroll all the way to the top to take action. The button should either jump up to the top where they can fill out the fields or trigger a popup window where they can enter their info into the fields.
7.

Date and time of event

a. Is this accurate and consistent throughout the webpage? You might have it as December 5 and 6 initially, then moved your date, and forgot to update all date and time references on the webpage.
b. Is the time zone stated? Optional, but sometimes it's nice to state in both your local time zone and the majority of your target audience's timezone.
c.

Is it concise? Being concise on the date and time is important because it reduces characters/text, which is particularly important for mobile and getting the important stuff above the fold.

i. Instead of saying "December 5 and December 6", you can say "December 5-6".
ii. Exclude the year - Let's say it's December 2022 and you're promoting your event on January 9-10, 2023. You don't have to say what year it is because by default you're referring to the upcoming/next January and not January 2024 or any other January in the future.
iii. Instead of saying "2:00 PM PST - 5:00 PM PST", you can say "2-5 PM PST".
8.

Major sections - The above the fold section is by far the most important, but also pay attention to the different sections you can have below it. You don't necessarily need all of them, but the more the merrier.

a. Section contrast - A nice way to indicate the different sections is to alternate background colors. In the IT factor example, it alternates from black to white as it changes sections.
b. Who is it for section - Describe your target audience and why they should attend.
c. About the workshop section - What will you learn at the workshop, what will you do during the workshop, and what makes your workshop different than others.
d. About you (speakers) section - A short bio of you and any guest speaker(s). Ideally, it should include why you are qualified to teach the event topic.
e. Testimonials section - Even if this is your first event, you can put testimonials from people who have worked with you directly or in the past. Videos, screenshots, or text of anything nice anyone has ever said about you and/or working with you. This also means that you should get testimonials from people who have attended previous events, especially if they didn't buy and got to attend the event for free. Be sure to ask for event testimonials after every event so that you can add them here.
9.

Calendar embed - For Webpage #1 - Warm Traffic Landing Page and Webpage #3 - Cold Traffic Call Booking Page, you'll need to embed a calendar for your prospect to book an interview call.

a. Does it embed and fully display well? It depends on your landing page builder, but Calendly (what we recommend) should display fully and well.
b. Is it clear on how to book the interview? It should be clear and obvious on what to click in the calendar to book. Typically that means clicking on a calendar date to see what time slots are available. In this example using Acuity, it's not very clear on what to click on next. If you're using Calendly, this shouldn't be an issue.
c. Is it easy to book the interview? Is the overall process for booking an interview easy and done with a minimal amount of clicks/friction. In the same Acuity example, the user needs to go through multiple screens to finalize a booking. If you're using Calendly, this shouldn't be an issue.
d. Does it have the minimal critical fields you need? At the least, it should collect their name and email, and phone number so you can send them email and text message reminders. You can add additional questions, but keep in mind that the more questions you ask, the more friction this will cause and lead to less likelihood of them following through and booking the call. If you don't have much lead volume, then you probably don't want to add any additional questions because you can just ask them when you get on the call. If you have a lot of lead volume (a huge audience and getting tons of traffic), then you may want to add questions for segmentation and qualification purposes. For example, you may ask how long they've been in business and if the answer is less than 1 year, then they might not be making much money and you might not take the call.
e. Does it do what it's supposed to after the interview is booked? After the interview is booked, you can either have the person stay on the same page or optionally redirect to Webpage #4 - Call Booked Success Page. Also, the person should be properly tagged in your email service as a result to indicate they are booked for an interview and don't need to receive any email sequence or call/text follow-ups.
10.

Does it look good on mobile? Make sure to load the webpage on your mobile phone to make sure it looks just as good on mobile as it does on desktop because often times there are changes to make after the initial build. Some of the biggest culprits:

a. Are the important parts above the fold as much as possible?
b. How's the padding?
c.

Do the graphics display properly?

i. Sometimes a horizontal banner might look good on desktop/laptop because the screen is wide, but bad on mobile because the screen is narrow.
ii. Another example is having a testimonial where the text is written onto the graphic with the person's face instead of having the face as a graphic on its own and the text on the webpage next to it. If the text is on the graphic, it might be near impossible or difficult to read on mobile because the entire graphic is shrunken down.
11.

Spelling and grammar - You will want to start writing your copy in something like Microsoft Word or Google Docs that has spellcheck so it can autocorrect any spelling or grammar mistakes on your initial drafts. Then from there when you're ready, you can have the text copy and pasted into your webpage. From that point, be careful of any revisions you make because your landing page builder might not have spellcheck.

12.

Removing template text - If you're using a template and copy pasting from there onto your webpage, you'll want to make sure all template text is removed.

13.

Page load speed - This typically isn't an issue, but it can be if you start adding a lot of images for testimonials. Either way, good to test your page load speed to ensure it's under 5 seconds, but ideally under 2 seconds.

14.

Do you have all your pixels installed for analytics, tracking, retargeting, etc.? This is more advanced, but for those who have paid traffic or a lot of traffic, you will want to add things like Google Analytics, hotjar, etc.
