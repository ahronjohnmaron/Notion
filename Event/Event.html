Event Date

So what date should you set for your 2-day event?

Ideally, they are on a Monday and Tuesday because that gives you the maximum number of business days after the event for enrollments (WThF).

You also probably want to avoid weekends because you’ll be competing with personal leisure time and family time. Which is more likely to happen: your prospect telling the family that they’re not attending their daughter’s soccer game on Saturday because they are going to stay home and attend a virtual event or your prospect shuffles some meetings around on Monday to attend?

How far ahead should you set the dates for your event?

You'll need about 3 weeks of promotion plus however long you need to get all your webpages and tech set up and tested. So for example, if today is January 1 and you estimate you'll need a month to get through all the learning material, build your event content, hire and train staff members, build out your pages, etc., that means you'll be fully ready to go around February 1. Then giving yourself 3 weeks of promotional time, that means your target event date is the first Monday after February 22 ish.

What dates should I avoid for my event?

You'll want to avoid setting your event on dates where your target market are unlikely to attend, such as big holidays (like July 4th if your audience is in the United States) and any niche specific days (maybe you're in B2B and there is a big industry event on that day).

In regards to December, the first week is fantastic because you'll get them before the holidays and you won't be competing with every other business owner who are kicking things off the first week of the new year. Otherwise, your next best event date is the second week/Monday of January.

The one caveat for the December timing is if you can only do it during the second week (even though it’s not optimal), it’s still worth doing (especially if it’s your first) and consider it a “soft launch” so that you can get the first-timer jitters out, get some live practice in, and going through the motions with a potentially smaller expected crowd. This is better than delaying until second week of January.

However, if you have a lot going on, this isn’t your first online rodeo, etc., then you’ll probably want to skip December (if you can’t do it in the earlier part) and resume in January.

Also, keep in mind of holidays or any niche specific days immediately after your event that would disturb your enrollment calls/period. For example, in the United States, Thanksgiving is a big holiday on Thursday and most people take that Friday off too. So even though Monday and Tuesday that week are technically clear for your event, you'll want to avoid that because then you can't do your enrollment calls on Thursday and Friday due to the Thanksgiving holiday.

How many days and how long should it be?

We tested all three: 3-day, 2-day, and 1-day virtual events (all being 3 hours long). The 2-day converts 50% better than the 1-day (so for every 2 enrollments from a 1-day, we get 3 instead [50% increase]) and there is no difference in enrollments in a 3-day versus 2-day (so you can save your time and energy). 1-day was not enough time for impact, trust, and connecting, so 2-day is the sweet spot for our market and price point.

This can vary for your market and pricing, so feel free to test, but we recommend generally to start with a 2-day event for 3 hours each.

What time should the event be?

This depends highly on your market and where they are located. Similar to date selection, you’ll want to pick a time that’s best for them to show up.

If they are entrepreneurs who make their own schedule, then they can probably do 2-5 PM after lunch.

Also, we are in Pacific Time, so if we do 2-5 PM, then even people in Eastern will be able to catch it at 5-8 PM their time. This is a note to keep in mind your target audience’s local time zone.

If your attendees have 9-5 jobs and it’s not work related, then you’ll probably want to do yours after work like 5-8 PM or 6-9 PM.

Instead of after lunch, you can try 9 AM - 12 PM. One benefit of doing it earlier is that you can even do feedback calls immediately after on Tuesday starting at say 1230 or 1 PM.

Event Topic

So what topic should you do for your event? Below is a list of a few different perspectives you can take when coming up with your topic. Keep in mind they're not mutually exclusive and there will likely be some overlap with the topic you ultimately decide upon.

1.
If you have existing clients in a paid coaching program or membership, make the topic content relevant and useful for them or something they've asked for. So in the absolute worst case scenario that you don't get one single new prospect into your event, at least your content will have served your existing clients.
2.
Make the topic something that new prospects will love and benefit from (even if it's not what they might not necessarily need right now at this very moment). It's having the right bait to catch fish. Sometimes your prospects really need A, but they want to learn about B because it's the latest buzz and that's what will get them to attend. Even though it's tempting to make the event topic A, you might want to make it B because that's what's going to lure them in (and you can still cover A if you want during your event). You want a topic that will be attractive to new prospects to bring them into your event.
3.
Let's say your coaching program is equivalent to first-grade school, then your event can be like kindergarten. It's sort of like a prerequisite to your paid program. You're training them on something that nicely leads them into your paid program. Another perspective to this is that your event solves Problem A for them. However, now they have Problem B as a result. (It’s the next obstacle in the way.) Your paid program should solve Problem B. For example, our target market may be coaches selling sessions by the hour and are burnt out (Problem A), so they come to our event to learn how to escape the hourly model and sell $5k+ programs. Naturally their new Problem B next is they don’t know how to sell their new $5k+ program (Problem B), which our paid program solves (and more). An added benefit to this approach is that you've accelerated the onboarding process and set them up for success in your paid program by getting some helpful if not necessary foundational training done beforehand.
4.
Teach them some of the content from your paid program by brainstorming through the 3 main categories in your triangle model and its 3 sub-categories to come with up with the topic and content for your live event so that you give your attendees a little preview and taste of what they get when they buy the paid program.

At the end of the day regardless of what topic you teach, don’t hold back and teach them as much as they need to know in your event for them to get a breakthrough experience because that will tremendously help you get them into your paid program while impacting lives at the same time. You also want them to feel like the information you're teaching isn't basic and instead feel premium.
