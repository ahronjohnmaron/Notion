Enrollment Calls

After the event is over, it's time for your Enrollment Specialist (ES) to take over with the feedback calls aka enrollment calls or sales calls!

This is a general outline of how the enrollment calls will go. Please also see the Closer’s Playbook for more details and training on sales, handling objections, etc.

Reinforcing the Feedback Call During the Pitch/Event

Before we jump into talking about the feedback calls, we must emphasize again the importance of telling the event attendees during the event (at the end of the pitch) to honor and attend the feedback calls they have scheduled already even if they don't think they're ready to sign up for your program. You can review that scripting in Part 7 - Followup Calls section on the Pitch page.

That part of the pitch is critical because it will affect the show-up rate for the feedback calls and minimize cancellations and rescheduling.

Confirming the Feedback Call

Ok now that we've gotten that disclaimer of the way, let's look at a best practice of sending manual text messages confirming the feedback call to further increase the showup rate.

The calendar booking service for the feedback call will send out automated email and text message reminders, but you should also send out a manual text message confirming the call.

Each morning, the ES should look at their calendar, see all the appointments they have for the day, and personally text each person a personalized video to confirm the appointment. Here's what that video and text can look like:

Hey [NAME]!

This is [ES NAME] here from [SPEAKER'S] team. I'm here in my office looking at my calendar and see we have our feedback call scheduled for today. Wooohoooo!

I remember our first connection call and what we talked about [THROW IN SOMETHING PERSONAL YOU KNOW ABOUT THAT PERSON BASED ON YOUR NOTES OR WHAT YOU REMEMBERED ABOUT THEM], so I'm excited to hear how the event impacted you and what you learned!

I have a back-to-back call with another person right after our call together, so it'll be incredible if we can connect on time so that we can make the most of our time together. So just want to make sure that [TIME OF CALL] still works for you?

After sending the video, follow up with text:

Hey [NAME]! It's [ES NAME] here from [SPEAKER'S] team. Made the quick video above for you 😊 also wanted to confirm that [TIME OF CALL] still works for you today because I have another call right after?

Using a personalized video and text will increase the likelihood of your prospect showing up for your sales call. We know it takes a little extra effort to make these videos and send these confirmations, but also remember how expensive it is (losing a $5k+ sale or however much your program is) if your prospect doesn't show up for their call and you need to follow up and chase. This is an expensive part of the process where you don't want to be lazy or cut corners and instead go all out in making sure they show up for the call.

Objections & Challenges

A natural part of the sales process will be encountering objections and challenges and it is very important you're able to handle them.

By going through the above, you should have helped them isolate and address all their questions, concerns, and objections and they should want to join your program. The only thing remaining at this point is sorting out the finances.

Scholarships and Price Floor

One of the great things about offering group coaching is that there is a relatively small variable cost to adding a new customer. That means the cost of fulfillment for 9 customers in group coaching is pretty close to fulfillment for 10 customers. Your Kajabi bill isn't going to go up nor will it cost you any more time if 10 people are sitting in your Zoom weekly training versus 9. Thus, it's advantageous to get as many customers as you can by being flexible on your price because it doesn't cost you much to fulfill the service.

So if your program is $10k and it costs you an extra $100 for fulfillment (the extra time it takes for administrative and accounting work) and your prospect can only afford $9k, would you turn them down because you're adamant on $10k or would you be flexible on your price?

The way we elegantly give discounts is phrasing it as a "scholarship" like a university would give to a student. "Hey, I love your attitude, what you're doing, and how much you want to be part of our program. Instead of the full $10k for the program, what if you had a $1k scholarship so that you're only paying $9k? Would you be able to do that?"

This is also where the concept of "price floor" comes in. It's the minimum (floor) that you're willing to accept and have authorized your ES to slowly go down to if necessary to make the sale. Economically speaking, if you know your additional cost of fulfillment is $100 for that customer, any price you can negotiate above that amount is profitable. Obviously you don't want your floor that low, but however you determine and set the price floor amount, let your ES know so they have the authority to go that low and negotiate to make the sale.

And when making the concessions as the ES, don’t just jump to the price floor as the first counteroffer. Go in increments. For more details and advanced strategy, check out the Ackerman bargaining method.

Payment Plan

With that said on price and price floor, remember that price is just one element when it comes to finances. You also have terms. For example, would you buy a house in a desirable location for $100M? Probably not with that information alone. But what if it was $1 down and $1/mo until paid off (in 99,999,999 months)? The terms on that payment plan are so good that the $100M price didn't matter.

In our scenario, the 3 main other variables you have in addition to price are: the upfront payment amount (the down payment), the amount of the subsequent (monthly) payments, and the number of subsequent (monthly) payments.

And that's the thing about how most people think when it comes to making a larger purchase. They often think more about those 3 variables than the price. But when it really comes down to it, it’s mostly 2 variables: they want to know how much they need to pay now (put down as a deposit) and how much are their monthly payments. Thus by knowing what most people care about and how they think about money in this context, you have the flexibility to play with those variables and negotiate something that works for them and you to make the sale.

Of course the ideal situation is to collect 100% of your price as 100% of the upfront payment. For example, you pitch during your event that it's $10k for your 6-month coaching program and someone pays the full $10k upfront.

Most customers are unable to or don't do that, so it's up to you to provide a payment plan option (usually included in the pitch) that you can use as an alternative to the full price. Use scholarships when necessary, collect as much as you can upfront (so they have skin in the game and you have less to seller finance and risk not being able to collect), and do your best not to extend payments beyond the term of the coaching (if your initial package is 6 months long, try not to go past 6 monthly payments, especially if you intend on selling renewals to keep the client in your mastermind on an ongoing basis).

Still Not Able to Pay

First off, as much as you try to only let financially qualified people into the event, there will still be people who aren't in a good financial place and slip into the event. Even though that's the case, we can still continue to hone your customer avatar and improve your marketing to better find and attract the right people.

If you have a large audience and it's easy to get people into your event, you may even want to have a form they need to fill out during the registration process that will help filter out financially unqualified people even before doing the interview.

Remember that money isn't the only value you can exchange. Perhaps that person has a skill that you're willing to barter in exchange for access to your group coaching. Or maybe they are willing to do anything and have a fantastic attitude that you can give them a full scholarship because they will make a great intern or apprentice. Remember your variable cost for each additional new client is very low, so you can be flexible and creative.

You can also downsell into a lower priced offer. Some people sell just access to the hub and recordings and they get no live coaching. That is a fantastic option because it is practically 100% pure profit.

Another thing you can do, whether they can pay or not, is ask for a testimonial for the event. This is especially a great strategy for those feedback calls where you're 20 minutes in and you don't think a sale is possible, but you have another 10 minutes left for the call, get a testimonial from them. If the Zoom call isn't already recording, start recording and ask questions to elicit positive responses so that you can use the video, audio, and/or text as a testimonial on the event registration page.

Taking Payment

Once you have agreed to terms on the payment, it's time to collect credit card details verbally over Zoom so that you can process the transaction for them. We recommend using Stripe as your payment processor. Here are some options on how to take payment based on your situation:

  1. If you, the business owner, is doing the sales calls and taking your customer's payment, then you can log into Stripe and process the payment. If you are outsourcing the enrollments to someone else AND you don't mind that person seeing all your Stripe payments and customers, then you can add them as a member of your Stripe account as a Support Specialist. If you do NOT want them to see that information, then move onto the next option below.
  2. This method requires a little technical expertise, but you can build a payment form like this using Paperform and connect it to your Stripe account so that the ES can process the payment on the form and the credit card information securely goes into your Stripe account and you can set them up for future payments without having to obtain the customer's credit card information again. If you have a WordPress site, WP Simple Pay could be a good option.
  3. If you are not very technical and won't be setting up the payment form, you can have a customers choose what to pay Stripe payment link created for the ES to take the upfront payment; however, the credit card information won't automatically save into Stripe to process future payments, so the ES will need to note the credit card information in the event tracker spreadsheet (do not email because it's much less secure) and comment/assign to your accounting person so they know to later enter it into Stripe for processing future payments. Here is a video that walks you through that.
  4. If processing payments is too complicated for the ES, they can skip the upfront charge and just enter the credit card information into the event tracker spreadsheet and comment/assign to your admin so they know to create a payment in Stripe and process the credit card.
  5. If the client wants to use PayPal funds to pay, be sure to give your ES your PayPal email address to give to the client to send. From there, you will want to make your accounting/admin person a user in your PayPal account with these permission settings so they can handle the accounting without getting full access to your PayPal account.

From here, you should have the customer's credit card information saved into Stripe and they've been processed for the upfront payment. To do the future payments, if it's the same amount every month ($500/mo for 6 months for example), you can create a $1 product in Stripe and create a monthly subscription plan. If it's not the same amount every month ($500 in 30 days, $750 in 60 days, etc.), then you will have to mark your calendar and manually process that as a payment when the date comes around.

New Client Onboarding

The final aspect of a (closed) sales call is to send the new client that information for the first onboarding meeting. You'll want to send them the onboarding instruction email with the Zoom meeting information and a calendar invite with the date and time.

You can do both of these manually or do what the interviewer did using the calendar booking service for the live event and enrollment calls by booking them into an onboarding event in the calendar booking service, which can automatically trigger that onboarding email, send out the calendar invite, and send them an automated email and text reminders as the date and time approaches.

End of Call Best Practices

Whether they enrolled or not, some best practices to do near the end of the call (especially if you don't have a back-to-back) are:

  1. Ask for a referral - See who they know who could benefit from attending your next virtual event. Or alternatively:
  2. Ask if they want to get paid to promote your virtual event - Particularly if they didn't enroll due to finances, you may want to ask if they like to join your referral program and make money from referring people to your virtual event.
  3. Get a testimonial - Your feedback calls should be on autorecord, which makes it convenient for getting a testimonial. This is a great time to ask if it's ok to get a testimonial from them, which is really just asking them a few guided questions to elicit the answers you want. Also ask if they're ok with the testimonial being in video. If yes, you can use the clip. If not, pull the transcribe and use those words. Whether you explicitly do this at the end or not, there may be parts of your feedback call at the beginning where your guest said great things about their experience at your event and it'd be asking if it's ok to use those words (or video) as a testimonial.
